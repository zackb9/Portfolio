3 A Simple Model¶

3.1¶

$\alpha_i$ is the attack score for the home team. $\beta_i$ is the defense score for the home team. $\gamma$ is a constant parameter representing the general scoring rate of the league as a whole. $\eta$ is the 'home advantage' score.

3.2¶

We need to have constraints on the parameters to make the model identifiable and ensure it has a unique solution. For example, we could make the constraint that $\sum \alpha_i =0$ and $\sum \beta_i =0$, so that they represent the difference in a teams attack and defense compared with the league average, which is represented by the constant $\gamma$. The model assumes that the home and away teams' scoring rates are independent, and that the goals scored by each team in different matches are independent. It also assumes the attack or defense scores are independent of the opponent (e.g. doesn't take into account that teams may defend worse against their local rivals because of added pressure, etc).

3.3¶

I use Nelder-Mead optimization to maximise the log-likelihood.

# log-likelihood function for the model
def log_likelihood(params):
    alpha = params[:nteams]
    beta = params[nteams:2*nteams]
    gamma, eta = params[2*nteams], params[2*nteams+1]
    lambda_k = np.exp(alpha[i-1] + beta[j-1] + gamma + 0.5*eta)
    mu_k = np.exp(alpha[j-1] + beta[i-1] + gamma - 0.5*eta)
    return np.sum(x*np.log(lambda_k) - lambda_k - np.log(scipy.special.factorial(x)) +
                  y*np.log(mu_k) - mu_k - np.log(scipy.special.factorial(y)))

# initial parameter values
init_params = np.zeros(2*nteams+2)

# optimization using the Nelder-Mead algorithm

The MLE for $\gamma$ is 0.268, the MLE for $\eta$ is 0.370, the maximum value of the log likelihood is -3967. The MLEs for the $\alpha$ and $\beta$ parameters are given in the table above. Interpreting them, we see that the baseline number of goals for two equally matched teams on a neutral ground is 0.268 goals each. Home advantage increases this to $0.268+0.370/2= 0.453$ for the home team and decreases it to $0.268-0.370/2= 0.0.083$ for the away team. This means that for equally matched sides, the home team is expected to score 0.453 goals per game while the away team would just score 0.083 goals per game, which makes sense as it is widely reported that 'home advantage' has a significant impact on football scores. Looking at the estimates for $\alpha$ (the attack score), we see that these make sense in general. The teams with the highest $\alpha$ value are LA Galaxy, New York Red Bulls and Seattle Sounders, who are the top 3 for goals scored per game. Similarly, the teams with the highest $\beta$ parameter (the defence score, remembering that more negative means stronger defense) are Toronto FC and CF Montreal, who are two of the sides with the highest goals conceded per game.

4 Model Extensions¶

4.1¶

  • One extension to this model could be to consider how the home advantage effect might vary across different teams. Some teams might have a stronger home advantage than others (Anfield comes to mind), and this could be captured by introducing additional parameters in the model which represented a personalised home advantage for each team, similar to the attack and defence score.
  • Instead of treating each game as an independent event, we can incorporate time dynamics into the model. This can be done by introducing a time-dependent variable that captures the evolution of team strengths over time. We could use recent match results to build a dynamic 'form' parameter (e.g. number of wins, draws, and losses in the last 5 matches). Alternatively, we could assume that team strengths follow a random walk over time. This would allow us to capture changes in team strengths due to factors such as injuries, team composition, and changes in coaching staff.
  • Interaction terms: The current model assumes that the effects of home advantage, team quality, and opponent quality are independent of each other. However, we could include interaction terms that capture how these factors interact with each other. For example, we could include an interaction term between home advantage and attack score to capture how good teams tend to perform better at home. Another useful interaction term would be between home attack score and away defense score, to capture how strong attacking teams score even more against weaker defensive teams.
  • Non-linear effects: The model could be extended to allow for non-linear relationships between the predictors and the outcome variable. For example, it could be that a team's home advantage has a non-linear relationship with their goal-scoring rate so we should include non-linear terms such as quadratic or cubic functions of the home advantage parameter. #### 4.2 - Adding recent form to the model To do this, we can start by defining a window of the most recent matches to consider (e.g., the last 5 matches). For each team, we can then calculate their recent form as a set of summary statistics based on their recent results and goal difference. For example, we could calculate the number of wins, losses, and draws in their last 5 matches, as well as their average goal difference in those matches.

Once we have calculated these summary statistics for each team, we can add them as additional predictor variables in the Poisson regression model. We can then estimate the coefficients for these new variables in the Poisson regression model, which will give us an idea of the impact of a team's recent form on their scoring rates. If we want to prioritize interpretability and readability, we can combine these statistics into one 'team form' feature, though it is not clear whether a linear or nonlinear combination would be better, so leaving them as separate statistics is preferable.

In terms of implementation, there would be a couple of issues to address. Firstly, how to deal with filling in these statistics for teams that have not yet played 5 matches. My instinct is to leave the values as zero until they have played 5 games so that, for example, a single large win does not incorrectly give a team a huge 'form' score and give an overly optimstic prediction for their next game. We would also have to accept that this model can only predict games if we have data for the previous 5 matches and so would not be appropriate for some use cases. For example, it would not be appropriate to predict the final league standings before the season has begun, as we would be predicting results using form data that is only based on our model's predictions, not real results. Finally, we would need to evaluate the performance of the model with and without the new predictor variables to see if they improve the model's predictive accuracy.

4.3¶

  • Weather conditions: temperature, wind, speed and precipitation all have an impact on how football is played. Having played for teams since 6 years old, I don't need a model to know that it is both harder to defend and score in the snow, and so I am sure that weather indicators will have a significant relationship with goals scored.
  • Team morale/fan sentiment: this is a difficult feature to capture objectively, though some NLP with Twitter data could be useful. I am currently working on a small project classifying tweet sentiment using machine learning, and a similar approach could be used here to generate a feature which could potentially indicate the mental state of the team before each match. This could be useful as it is difficult to incorporate manager changes, player transfers and injuries into the model, though this obviously can have a large impact on results.

4.4¶

To add seasonality to Model A, we add a parameter, $\omega$, which is a vector of length 10 (the season runs from March to December) in which each element represents the change in scoring rate based on the month. Our new formulas for the log scoring rate are: $\begin{align} \ln\lambda_k &= \alpha_{i(k)} + \beta_{j(k)} + \gamma + \frac{\eta}{2} + \omega_m \\ \ln\mu_k &= \alpha_{j(k)} + \beta_{i(k)} + \gamma - \frac{\eta}{2} + \omega_m \end{align}$ where $\omega_m$ is interpreted as the increase or decrease in scoring rate for that month, with $m \in \{3,...,12\}$. Interpreting the coefficients that we get for $\omega$, the graph below the code helps us to see that they are in line with our intuition. The general pattern of average goals per month is the same as that of the omega values. I used AIC and BIC to evaluate the models. These both penalise extra parameters and evaluate the goodness of fit of a model. Model A had the lower value for both AIC and BIC, and so is the better model.

6.1¶

To simulate matches I use np.random.poisson to sample from the Poisson distribution. The parameters are the expected number of goals scored by each team according to my model.

In [ ]:
def simulation():
    # create a dictionary to store the expected points of each team
    expected_points = {}
    expected_goals = {}
    expected_diff = {}

    # loop over all the matches in the test set
    for k in range(len(test)):
        home_team = test.iloc[k]['Home']
        away_team = test.iloc[k]['Away']
        lambda_k = lambda_k_test[k]
        mu_k = mu_k_test[k]
        
        # simulate goals, update goals scored and goal difference
        home_goals = np.random.poisson(lambda_k)
        away_goals = np.random.poisson(mu_k)

        expected_goals[home_team] = expected_goals.get(home_team, 0) + home_goals
        expected_goals[away_team] = expected_goals.get(away_team, 0) + away_goals
        expected_diff[home_team] = expected_diff.get(home_team, 0) + home_goals - away_goals
        expected_diff[away_team] = expected_diff.get(away_team, 0) + away_goals - home_goals
        
        # update the points for each team based on the match result
        if home_goals > away_goals:
            expected_points[home_team] = expected_points.get(home_team, 0) + 3
        elif home_goals == away_goals:
            expected_points[home_team] = expected_points.get(home_team, 0) + 1
            expected_points[away_team] = expected_points.get(away_team, 0) + 1
        else:
            expected_points[away_team] = expected_points.get(away_team, 0) + 3

            
    combined_dict = {}
    for key in expected_points.keys():
        combined_dict[key] = {'Expected Points': expected_points[key], 
                            'Expected Goal Diff': expected_diff[key],
                            'Expected Goals': expected_goals[key],
                            'Conference': team_conferences[2015][key]}
    simulated_table = pd.DataFrame.from_dict(combined_dict).T.rename_axis('Team').reset_index().sort_values(by=['Expected Points', 'Expected Goal Diff', 'Expected Goals', 'Team'], ascending=[False, False, False, True])
    Eastern_sim = simulated_table[simulated_table['Conference']=='Eastern']
    Western_sim = simulated_table[simulated_table['Conference']=='Western']
    
    return Western_sim, Eastern_sim

6.3¶

The simulations are all independent and identically distributed, and the probability of LA Galaxy finishing in the top 2 for each simulation is constant (the parameters remain constant for each simulation). The confidence interval is given by $\begin{equation} \hat{p} \pm Z_{0.975}\sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}} \end{equation}$

Thus, our precision is going to be equivalent to $Z_{0.975}\sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}$. Rearranging this and plugging in our values, we have $\begin{equation} n = 1.96^2\cdot\frac{0.2679(1-0.2679)}{0.0001^2} = 75,345,143 \end{equation}$
